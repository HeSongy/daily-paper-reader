# Lost in Time? Continuous Symmetry and Identifiability in Aided Inertial Navigation with Unknown Measurement Delays 

Jonathan Kelly 1, Phone Thiha Kyaw 1, and Matthew Giamou 2

Abstract — In many multisensor systems, measurements from different sensors are subject to unknown relative time delays. Accurate state estimation requires that delays be accounted for and, when possible, calibrated online. We consider the case of aided navigation, where measurements from a single aiding sensor are subject to an unknown but constant delay relative to the inertial measurement stream, and study the identifiability of the resulting system. Critically, identifiability depends not only on the temporal structure of the measurements, but also on the shape of the vehicle trajectory: some trajectories are sufficiently informative to support unique recovery of the delay and the navigation state, while others are not. Using the special Galilean group, we characterize these uninformative (or degenerate) trajectories and relate them to a continuous symmetry of the delayed measurement model, providing geometric insight into identifiability failures. We show that the class of trajectories for which identifiability fails is larger than previously reported, and connect our characterization to the familiar linearized, Jacobian-based analysis. Although our development is moti-vated by aided navigation, the underlying ideas apply more broadly to estimation problems on Lie groups with delayed measurements. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

An aided inertial navigation system (INS) fuses inertial measurements with data from additional sensors to estimate the state of a moving vehicle, typically including its po-sition, orientation, and velocity. The inertial measurement unit (IMU) supplies high-rate angular velocity and specific-force readings that drive the process model; lower-rate aiding sensors, such as GNSS receivers or cameras, provide ob-servations that correct drift to maintain bounded error over longer time horizons. In practice, measurements from different sensors are sub-ject to relative time offsets, or delays. These delays arise from sensing, processing, and communication latency, and are often modelled as constant but unknown [1]–[3]. For optimal navigation performance, delays must be properly accounted for within the state estimator. Failure to do so can lead to biased state estimates, inconsistent covariance estimates, and, in the worst case, divergence of the navigation solution. The estimation problem for a single aiding sensor is therefore to recover the unknown delay, the navigation state, and possibly other parameters, from the IMU and delayed aiding sensor measurements.

Online delay estimation is desirable because it avoids time-consuming offline calibration procedures. One tech-nique is to augment the estimator state with the delay parameter and apply a standard nonlinear estimation al-gorithm, such as the extended Kalman filter [2], [4], [5]. This approach is attractive because it fits easily within existing filtering frameworks. However, reliable online delay estimation requires that the augmented system be identifiable 

(or observable, in the usual state-estimation sense): the delay and the navigation state must be uniquely determined by the available measurement history. We previously [6] showed that recursive filters suffer from structural issues that lead to biased and inconsistent estimates. The present work addresses the question of whether the required information is present in the measurements at all, and explains the main issue with recursive updates. Processing measurements individually yields an unidentifiable system since, as we shall see, a symmetry of the measurement model prevents unique recovery of the delay and the navigation state. In this paper, we study identifiability in aided inertial navigation under measurement delays, and ask when the vehicle motion itself prevents unique recovery of the delay and the navigation state. We call motions for which unique recovery fails uninformative trajectories .1 Along uninfor-mative trajectories, joint online estimation may become ill-

Our analysis relies on the special Galilean group SGal(3) .This 10-dimensional Lie group is a natural state space for inertial motion, combining position, velocity, orientation, and time in a single geometric structure. Its Lie algebra contains the space of IMU-driven inputs, that is, angular velocity and linear acceleration. This structure allows delay identifiability to be examined directly in terms of the vehicle trajectory and the delayed measurement history. The central contribution of the paper is a symmetry-based characterization of identifiability in aided inertial navigation. We show that the family of uninformative trajectories is determined by a continuous symmetry induced by Galilean transformations that leaves the measurement history un-changed. Because the symmetry is geometric, the resulting characterization gives a clearer picture of why identifiability fails than a purely linearized, Jacobian-based analysis alone. Our symmetry-based formulation reveals a larger class of uninformative trajectories than previously reported in the literature. Although our presentation is motivated by aided inertial navigation, the same ideas apply more broadly to kinematic systems in which delayed measurements are used to correct a propagated state. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. We first review background and related work, then introduce the identifiability and symmetry framework. Next, we develop the SGal(3) aided-navigation model and characterize the resulting family of uninformative trajectories. We close with implications of the analysis and directions for future work. II. B ACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK 

Structural identifiability, the question of whether unknown parameters can be recovered from noiseless output data, has a long history in systems theory [9], [10]. Observability is closely related, but concerns recovery of a time-varying state rather than fixed parameters; for nonlinear delay-free systems, it is typically assessed via rank-based tests involving Lie derivatives or observability Gramians [11], [12]. In the delayed aided-navigation setting we consider, the two viewpoints coincide. As we explain in Section V, retaining the IMU input history turns the trajectory into a function of fixed unknowns: the initial navigation state, the delay, and any additional parameters such as biases. 2

This is the same parameterization used implicitly by filters, smoothers, and sliding-window optimizers, which propagate an uncertain state estimate using the IMU inputs and compare the resulting predictions with aiding measurements. For this reason, we use the language of identifiability throughout. Our approach is to determine when the measurement model admits a nontrivial continuous symmetry: a trans-formation of the unknown parameters that leaves the out-puts unchanged. A nontrivial symmetry means that distinct parameter values generate the same output history. Mar-tinelli [13], [14] developed this idea for non-delayed systems   

> 2We refer to the initial navigation state as the initial condition in the sequel, to match the notation of (1).

using Lie derivatives, leading to symmetries characterized by local differential conditions. In our case, the symmetries act on the entire measurement history, and whether they are trivial depends on the shape of the trajectory over time. Yang et al. [7], [8] described specific uninformative trajectories for delayed visual-inertial systems through a Jacobian-based analysis; our work gives a more general characterization using the symmetry structure of the special Galilean group. Although we do not study estimator performance here, uninformative trajectories are of practical importance. In a related observability setting, Huang [15] showed that appar-ent information may be gained along unobservable (uniden-tifiable) directions in the state (parameter) space, leading to estimator inconsistency and possibly to divergence. A. Distinguishability, Identifiability, and Observability 

We take the standard approach to determining identi-fiability based on output distinguishability [9], [10]. The definitions below are adapted to our delayed-measurement setting. As is standard in structural identifiability analysis, we treat the system in (1) as noise-free. Let [ts, t s + T ] be the observation interval, with ts ≥

τu. For a known input history sufficient to propagate the state from the initial time to ts + T , each parameter tuple 

X ∈ P determines an output history over [ts, t s + T ].Changing the delay τ shifts which points along the trajectory enter the measurement model, making delay identifiability a trajectory-dependent question. Definition 1 (Indistinguishability) . Let T ⊆ [ts, t s +T ] be a set of observation times. The parameter tuples X ◦, X ⋆ ∈ P 

are said to be indistinguishable on T if, for the same known input history, they produce identical outputs at every time in 

T . The corresponding delayed states may occur at different times along the trajectory, since the delays in X ◦ and X ⋆

need not be equal. Otherwise, the parameter tuples are said to be distinguishable on T .

Definition 2 (Identifiability) . A parameter tuple X ⋆ ∈ P is 

identifiable on a set of observation times T ⊆ [ts, t s + T ] if it is distinguishable on T from every X ∈ P with X̸ = X ⋆.The preceding definition is global: it requires uniqueness over the entire admissible parameter space P. For nonlinear systems, this can be difficult to establish directly and is often stronger than necessary. We therefore use a local notion, which requires uniqueness only within a neighbourhood of the parameters under consideration. 

Definition 3 (Local identifiability) . A parameter tuple X ⋆ ∈P is locally identifiable on a set of observation times T ⊆ 

[ts, t s + T ] if there exists a neighbourhood V of X ⋆ such that X ⋆ is distinguishable on T from every X ∈ V with 

X̸ = X ⋆. Equivalently, any X ∈ V that produces the same outputs as X ⋆ at every time in T must satisfy 

X = X ⋆.

Local identifiability can be assessed by linearizing the measurement model with respect to the unknown parameters. Each measurement contributes a Jacobian block, and these blocks are stacked to form the complete measurement Jaco-bian. Full column rank of the stacked Jacobian implies that the linearized measurements locally constrain the unknown parameters to first order, while rank deficiency indicates first-order perturbation directions that remain unconstrained. 

B. Continuous Symmetries 

We now state the symmetry principle used later in the pa-per. Our interest is in a continuous family of transformations of the unknown parameters that leaves the delayed output history unchanged over the observation interval. 

Theorem 1 (Symmetry implies unidentifiability) . Let X ⋆ ∈P. Suppose there exists ϵ > 0 and a smooth one-parameter family of transformations Sα : P → P , α ∈ (−ϵ, ϵ ), such that S0(X ) = X for all X in a neighbourhood of X ⋆.Suppose further that, for every α ∈ (−ϵ, ϵ ), the parameter tuples X ⋆ and Sα(X ⋆) are indistinguishable on [ts, t s + T ].If Sα(X ⋆)̸ = X ⋆ for all α ∈ (−ϵ, ϵ ) \ { 0}, then X ⋆ is not locally identifiable on [ts, t s + T ].

Proof. For every α ∈ (−ϵ, ϵ ), the tuples X ⋆ and Sα(X ⋆) are indistinguishable on [ts, t s + T ]. By continuity, Sα(X ⋆) →

X ⋆ as α → 0, so every neighbourhood of X ⋆ contains some 

Sα(X ⋆)̸ = X ⋆ producing the same output history. Hence the parameter-to-output map is not locally injective at X ⋆, and 

X ⋆ is not locally identifiable. In typical aided navigation systems, however, measure-ments are available only at discrete times. The same ar-gument applies directly to this case: if a nontrivial smooth family Sα leaves the outputs unchanged at the measurement times t1, . . . , t n, then the sampled parameter-to-output map is not locally injective. Thus, X ⋆ is not locally identifiable from the discrete measurements. Each symmetry family {S α} considered here is a local one-parameter group action on the parameter space. As the parameter α varies, the transformation Sα moves through parameter space while leaving the outputs unchanged; the resulting set is the symmetry orbit through X ⋆.

In this section, we develop a model for aided inertial navi-gation with delayed aiding measurements, making use of the special Galilean group. The process model is driven by IMU body-frame angular-rate and specific-force measurements. We treat the IMU biases as constant 3 and also assume a known constant gravity vector. We then study local identifia-bility using the traditional approach: we linearize the delayed measurement model and analyze the resulting Jacobian. This analysis identifies first-order perturbation directions that are not constrained by the delayed measurements, but it does not by itself explain their geometric origin. In Section VI, we show that these directions arise from a continuous symmetry of the delayed measurement model, giving a geometric characterization of uninformative trajectories.

VI. U NINFORMATIVE TRAJECTORIES 

We now arrive at the main result of the paper: there exists a family of trajectories for which the delay and initial condition cannot be uniquely recovered, regardless of the number or type of aiding measurements. The key insight is geometric: informativeness is determined by the shape of the trajectory, as encoded by the state transition matrix 

Φ(t). In particular, trajectories generated by a constant Lie algebra element are uninformative. For these trajectories, the delayed measurement model admits a continuous symmetry that leave the measurement history unchanged, as we make precise below.

C. Biases and Gravity 

We briefly clarify the role of inertial-sensor biases and gravity, neither of which changes the delay–initial-condition symmetry. The bias terms do not enter the symmetry in Theorem 2. Our prior work showed that the biases are locally weakly observable at each point along the trajectory [19]. Perturbations to the biases change the effective body-frame input history and therefore change the shape of the trajectory locally. For this reason, the biases are not part of the same symmetry orbit as the delay and initial condition, and their perturbations appear as independent directions in the linearized model. Gravity also does not alter the result. In our model, gravity is a known constant vector resolved in the inertial frame. Once the accelerometer bias and the known gravity contri-bution are accounted for, the trajectory shape is fixed by the corrected body-frame inputs. What matters for Theorem 2 is whether those inputs generate a trajectory whose shape (roughly speaking) is invariant under time shifts, that is, whether a constant generator exists for Φ(t). In this sense, gravity can be treated separately and does not affect the delay–initial-condition symmetry argument.

C ONCLUSION 

In this paper, we studied state and delay identifiability in aided inertial navigation with delayed measurements, using the special Galilean group SGal(3) . This formulation explicitly links the aided-navigation kinematics, delay, initial condition, and measurement history. We described a contin-uous symmetry of the delayed measurement model under Galilean transformations and connected the symmetry to the nullspace of the measurement Jacobian. Our main result is a geometric characterization of unin-formative trajectories. Identifiability depends not only on the number and type of measurements, but also on the shape of the vehicle trajectory itself. When the motion is generated by a constant element of the Lie algebra sgal (3) , the trajectory evolves along a one-parameter subgroup of SGal(3) , and a change in delay can be absorbed by a matching change in the initial condition, leaving the delayed measurement history unchanged. The resulting symmetry orbit contains indistinguishable sets of parameters, so the initial condition and delay cannot be uniquely recovered. This reveals a larger class of uninformative trajectories than previously reported in the literature, including several known cases as well as more general motions. There are several directions for future work. One is to ex-tend the analysis to relative measurements, including SLAM-type problems in which an additional spatial transformation appears in the measurement model [13]. Another is full spatiotemporal calibration, where unknown spatial extrinsic parameters and delays are estimated together.

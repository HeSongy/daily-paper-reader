Abstract 

Interpreting ultra-high-resolution (UHR) remote sensing images requires models to search for sparse and tiny visual evidence across large-scale scenes. Existing remote sensing vision-language models can inspect local regions with zooming and cropping tools, but most exploration strategies follow either a one-shot focus or a single sequential trajectory. Such single-path exploration can lose global context, leave scattered regions unvisited, and revisit or count the same evidence multiple times. To this end, we propose GeoVista , a planning-driven active perception framework for UHR remote sensing interpretation. Instead of committing to one zooming path, GeoVista first builds a global exploration plan, then verifies multiple candidate regions through branch-wise local inspection, while maintaining an explicit evidence state for cross-region aggregation and de-duplication. To enable this behavior, we introduce APEX-GRO , a cold-start supervised trajectory corpus that reformulates diverse UHR tasks as Global-Region-Object interactive reasoning processes with a unified, scale-invariant spatial representation. We further design an Observe-Plan-Track mechanism for global observation, adaptive region inspection, and evidence tracking, and align the model with a GRPO-based strategy using step-wise rewards for planning, localization, and final answer correctness. Experiments on RSHR-Bench, XLRS-Bench, and LRS-VQA show that GeoVista achieves state-of-the-art performance. Code and dataset are available at GeoVista. 

1 Introduction 

Ultra-high-resolution (UHR) remote sensing images typically cover large geographic areas [ 1, 2 ], while task-relevant evidence may appear as sparse, tiny, and spatially scattered regions. This mismatch between scene scale and evidence scale makes UHR interpretation different from natural image understanding. A model is expected not only to recognize local visual patterns, but also to decide where to search, which regions warrant closer inspection, and how to integrate evidence observed at different scales. Therefore, UHR remote sensing tasks naturally call for multi-scale observation, progressive evidence acquisition, and sequential decision-making [3, 4]. Existing studies mainly address this challenge from two directions. One line of work reduces visual redundancy through one-shot region selection or static visual pruning, including text-guided visual pruning methods, GeoLLaVA-8K [ 5], and C2F [ 3 ]. These methods improve efficiency by selecting compact visual evidence, but their focusing decisions are usually made before reasoning proceeds and cannot be easily revised. Another line of work equips models with interactive observation operations, such as cropping, resolution adjustment, and local zooming, as in ZoomEarth [ 6] and GeoEyes [ 4 ]. This motivates a central question: how can a model decide where to look, at what scale to inspect, and when to stop, while coordinating global search, local verification, and evidence aggregation? To answer this question, we pro-pose GeoVista , a planning-driven active percep-tion framework for UHR remote sensing under-standing. Given a task and a global observation, GeoVista first generates a structured exploration plan over multiple candidate regions of interest. It then performs branch-wise verification, adap-tively re-focuses uncertain regions, and updates an explicit evidence state that records inspected locations, verified findings, and their spatial ref-erences. The final answer is produced by aggre-gating evidence across branches, which expands search coverage while preserving global context and reducing duplicated evidence. Training such a framework is challenging because standard VQA-style supervision provides only final answers and does not specify how a model should search across scales. We therefore construct 

APEX-GRO , a cold-start supervised trajectory corpus that teaches the model to express UHR tasks as cross-scale interactive reasoning processes with a discrete, scale-invariant spatial syntax across Global, Region, and Object levels. Based on this initialization, we introduce an Observe-Plan-Track mechanism to coordinate global observation, region planning, branch-wise inspection, and evidence tracking. Finally, we apply a GRPO-based alignment strategy with step-wise rewards for planning quality, localization progress, and final answer correctness. 2 Related Works 

Remote Sensing Vision-Language Models. Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs), including proprietary systems such as GPT-4 [ 7 ] and Gemini [ 8 ], as well as open-source model families such as LLaVA [ 9, 10 , 11 ] and Qwen-VL [ 12 , 13 , 14 ], have shown strong visual-language understanding capabilities. To adapt such models to aerial and satellite imagery, remote sensing VLMs align RS-specific visual encoders with large language models, leading to systems such as RSGPT [ 15 ], SkyEyeGPT [ 16 ], GeoChat [ 17 ], EarthMind [ 18 ], and EarthVL [ 19 ]. Recent high-resolution extensions, such as GeoLLaVA-8K [ 5], further enlarge the visual context for remote sensing images. However, most of these methods still treat UHR interpretation as a one-shot encoding problem, where a fixed visual token budget must balance global coverage and local granularity. This makes it difficult to capture tiny, sparse, and spatially distributed evidence in large-scale scenes. 

Tool-Interleaved Visual Reasoning. Beyond static image-text understanding, recent work has explored tool-interleaved visual reasoning, where models dynamically acquire visual evidence during the reasoning process. The “Thinking with Images” paradigm [ 20 ] extends textual Chain-of-Thought reasoning by inserting visual perception steps into intermediate reasoning trajectories. General-domain systems such as DeepEyes [ 21 ], SenseNova-MARS [ 22 ], Vision-R1 [ 23 ], and MedVR [ 24 ]further show that visual tool use and reinforcement learning can improve multi-step perception and evidence grounding. However, these works are mainly designed for natural images, documents, medical images, or synthetic reasoning tasks, and their data distributions are not directly aligned with UHR remote sensing imagery, where task-relevant evidence is often tiny, sparse, and distributed across large geographic areas. Meanwhile, traditional RS VQA datasets such as RSVQA [ 25 ] and EarthVQA [ 26 ] mainly provide single-turn question-answer supervision, offering limited guidance on how a model should search, zoom, and verify evidence across scales. 

Active Perception for UHR Remote Sensing. Active perception methods attempt to address the res-olution bottleneck by enabling models to select regions, adjust resolutions, or zoom into local details during inference. In remote sensing, methods such as ZoomEarth [ 6], and GeoEyes [ 4] demonstrate the effectiveness of interactive observation for UHR image interpretation. Related trajectory datasets, including LRS-GRO [ 6] and UHR-CoZ [ 4 ], further introduce multi-level annotations or interleaved reasoning trajectories for active perception. Nevertheless, existing approaches usually organize exploration as a single sequential path, which limits their ability to compare multiple Regions of Interest (ROIs), maintain global context, track verified evidence, and avoid missed or duplicated detections. In contrast, GeoVista introduces planning-driven multi-branch exploration through an Observe-Plan-Track mechanism, enabling global planning, recursive local verification, and evidence aggregation for UHR remote sensing understanding. 

3 Methodology 

Given an UHR remote sensing image I and a user query Q, our goal is to generate an answer A by actively acquiring and aggregating visual evidence across multiple scales. Instead of encoding the entire image in a single forward pass or following a single zooming trajectory, GeoVista formulates UHR interpretation as a planning-driven active perception process. The model first observes a global view, then plans multiple candidate ROIs, verifies them through branch-wise local inspection, and finally aggregates the verified evidence for prediction. This section introduces the three key components of GeoVista. First, we construct APEX-GRO ,a supervised trajectory corpus that teaches the model to conduct cross-scale interactive reasoning over Global, Region, and Object levels. Second, we present the Observe-Plan-Track inference framework, which enables global planning, recursive local verification, and explicit evidence-state tracking. Third, we describe a two-stage training pipeline that combines supervised fine-tuning with GRPO-based alignment for long-horizon active perception. 34. Quality Control

3.1 APEX-GRO: Cross-Scale Interleaved Trajectory Construction 

UHR remote sensing interpretation often requires more than a single image-question-answer pair: a model must decide where to inspect, invoke visual tools, update intermediate evidence, and continue exploration when local regions remain ambiguous. However, most existing RS VQA datasets provide only final-answer supervision and do not specify how cross-scale evidence should be acquired. To provide a cold start for this behavior, we construct APEX-GRO (Active Planning and 

EX ecution across Global-Region-Object levels), a supervised trajectory corpus for tool-interleaved UHR reasoning. 

Trajectory Format. Each trajectory in APEX-GRO is represented as a sequence of reasoning states, tool actions, visual observations, and evidence updates. τ = ( z0, a 0, o 1, z 1, a 1, o 2, . . . , z T , A ), where 

zt is the textual reasoning state, at is a visual action such as cropping or zooming, ot is the returned visual observation, and A is the final answer. We define each visual action as at = crop (bt, ℓ t),where bt is the selected ROI and ℓt ∈ { L0, L 1, L 2} denotes the global, region, or object level. In practice, trajectories are serialized with functional fields such as <tool_call> , <think> , and 

<answer> . Within <think> , sub-steps such as [Observation] , [Plan] , and [Track] are used to update evidence and monitor progress. This format provides supervision for both tool use and evidence aggregation. 

Data Construction Pipeline. As illustrated in Figure 3, APEX-GRO is synthesized through a four-stage pipeline. First, we collect diverse RS data sources, including DOTA-v1.5 [ 27 ] and FAIR1M [ 28 ] for visual grounding, AID [ 29 ], UC-Merced [ 30 ], NWPU-RESISC45 [ 31 ], and WHU-RS19 [ 32 ] for scene classification, GeoLLaVA-8K [ 5] for high-resolution spatial reasoning, and EarthVQA [ 26 ] for urban/rural categorization. Second, a context engine converts available annotations into a nested task checklist that specifies targets and sub-goals to be resolved. The checklist only guides task decomposition; exact oracle coordinates and final answers are hidden from the teacher model, reserved for automatic validation and human verification. Third, a teacher VLM (gemini-3.1-flash-lite-preview ) generates tool-interleaved trajectories by alternating among reasoning, planning, crop-based inspection, and evidence tracking. Finally, we apply quality control to remove trajectories with coordinate leakage, invalid tool syntax, malformed spatial references, or inconsistent final answers, and drop evaluation benchmark overlaps. 

Global-Region-Object Hierarchy. APEX-GRO organizes UHR perception into a three-level hierar-chy. At the Global level ( L0), the model observes a downsampled global view V 0

global and produces a macroscopic exploration plan. At the Region level ( L1), it inspects candidate ROIs V 1

crop to verify structural patterns and narrow down task-relevant areas. At the Object level ( L2), it performs fine-grained inspection V 2

crop for small targets, object attributes, and precise localization. This hierarchy encourages global coverage before local evidence acquisition and reduces unnecessary inspection of irrelevant regions.

Absolute pixel coordinates do not generalize well across image sizes, while unrestricted continuous coordinates are difficult to serialize reliably in language-model outputs. 3.2 GeoVista: Observe-Plan-Track Active Perception Framework 

GeoVista follows the same action space and spatial syntax as APEX-GRO during inference. Given an UHR image I and a query Q, it avoids greedily committing to a single zooming path. Instead, it first constructs a global plan over multiple ROIs, and then verifies these ROIs with local observations while maintaining a structured evidence state. Although all branches are decoded sequentially in one autoregressive trace, they are conditioned on a shared global plan and correspond to different planned ROIs. We therefore refer to this process as multi-branch exploration .

5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce GeoVista, an active perception framework for UHR remote sensing imagery. By replacing isolated single-path scanning with Planning-Driven Parallel Multi-Hop Exploration, it achieves seamless global-local integration with high precision. Furthermore, we constructed APEX-GRO, a rich interleaved reasoning training set that provides the crucial scale-invariant spatial syntax for initial supervised fine-tuning. We further aligned the agent using a customized GRPO pipeline. By applying multi-dimensional constraints and dynamic rewards, we forced the agent to "think" before acting, successfully internalizing a strict "Observe-Plan-Track" process. Extensive evaluations across RSHR-Bench, XLRS-Bench, and LRS-VQA demonstrate that GeoVista achieves state-of-the-art performance, conclusively proving that structured parallel reasoning and strict step-by-step adherence are the keys to unlocking complex scene understanding in expansive visual environments. 9References

A Data Construction Pipeline 

A.1 APEX-GRO Fine-grained Annotation Data Construction Pipeline 

To generate high-quality, leakage-free, end-to-end reasoning trajectories, APEX-GRO employs an automated and human-in-the-loop pipeline comprising four core modules.This pipeline aims to generate the Agent-Zoom-SFT dataset for model fine-tuning through systematic constraints and validation.The specific construction process is as follows: 

1. Data Collection & Difficulty Assessment 

The pipeline first collects samples from multi-source remote sensing datasets (e.g., DOTA-v1.5, FAIR1M, EarthVQA), covering diverse tasks such as counting, visual grounding, route planning, classification, and spatial relationships. To ensure data quality, the system introduces a Difficulty Assessment mechanism, categorizing the generated questions and images into two types: • Hard Cases: 100% manual labeling is adopted to ensure the absolute correctness of multi-level parallel zooming reasoning trajectories in complex scenarios. • Simple Cases: Rapidly processed through an Automated Labeling pipeline to extract target areas (VLM Regions). 

2. Context Engine & Constraints Injection 

After acquiring the foundational data, the context engine is responsible for generating structured prompts. This engine fuses three major input streams: • Trajectory Compiler: Converts raw coordinates into a Nested Checklist to provide structured targets. • Task-Specific SOP Template: Strictly defines the output format of the CoT. • Constraint Injection: Compulsorily injects behavioral constraints (Hide Oracle Knowledge) to ensure a Human Visual Simulation exploration process. 3. Multi-Turn Execution Loop 

In the multi-turn reasoning phase, the Teacher Inference model engages in closed-loop interaction based on the system prompt, the initial image, and the dialogue history. In each turn, the model first outputs a <Think> process to plan the current step, followed by initiating a <Tool Call> to invoke the zoom_in tool, passing in the relative bounding box coordinates. After the agent executes the call, the Cropped View is appended to the dialogue history as a new image. This "think-call-observe" loop continues, with the model continuously verifying task progress across multi-scale images until the Checklist Completed status is reached, ultimately outputting the <Answer> . 4. Quality Control & Leakage Sanitization 

To ensure the reliability and interpretability of the final SFT data, the generated trajectories must undergo rigorous quality filtering: • Leakage Sanitization: Rejects samples with Pre-action Coordinate Injection. • Structure Validation: Verifies that the data follows a legal interaction graph structure. 13 • Ground Truth Self-Consistency: Ensures the final answer is consistent with the annotated ground truth. After refinement and filtering through the aforementioned four stages, the pipeline ultimately con-structs the high-quality Agent-Zoom-SFT dataset (the detailed dataset size is shown in Table 4). At the onset of construction, we initially collected 142,125 raw data samples to serve as the foundation. Subsequently, utilizing this raw dataset, the system drove the model to generate the corresponding multi-turn interaction reasoning trajectories. Then, after undergoing the extremely rigorous leakage review, structural validation, and correctness verification within this module, a substantial amount of non-compliant generated data was discarded. Ultimately, the entire pipeline successfully distilled and retained 33,445 high-quality, fine-grained multi-turn interaction samples from the initial data.

D Training Details 

In this section, we provide the comprehensive hyperparameter settings and implementation details for both the Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and the Reinforcement Learning (RL) alignment phases to ensure full reproducibility. 

D.1 Phase 1: SFT 

To ensure a fair and rigorous comparison across all datasets in our ablation study, we apply an identical and standardized SFT configuration to the base Qwen2.5-VL-7B model. Specifically, we employ LoRA (rank=16, α=32, dropout=0.05) targeting all linear modules. The models are trained for 3 epochs using a cosine learning rate scheduler with a peak learning rate of 5 × 10 −5 and a 10% warmup ratio. To fully support the extensive context demands of our parallel multi-hop trajectories and UHR imagery, we expand the maximum sequence length to 16,384 tokens and cap the visual resolution at approximately 1M pixels ( image_max_pixels =1,003,520). The training process is further accelerated and memory-optimized utilizing bfloat16 precision, Flash Attention 2, and DeepSpeed ZeRO-2.

F Limitations and Future Work 

Although GeoVista has shown significant improvements in UHR remote sensing understanding, several limitations remain for future exploration. First, due to computational constraints, the rein-forcement learning phase was limited to two epochs. More extensive training with a larger exploration budget may yield further performance gains and deeper emergent agentic behaviors. Second, while our multi-dimensional reward design effectively guides the active perception process, we did not conduct an exhaustive ablation study to quantitatively isolate the exact contribution of each group reward component. Third, our current RL alignment focuses exclusively on verifiable reasoning tasks (Reasoning RL) such as grounding and counting. We have not explored yet a two-stage reinforcement learning pipeline that incorporates General RL for broader open-ended semantic alignment. G Broader Impacts 

This work advances ultra-high-resolution remote sensing understanding, offering significant positive societal impacts in domains such as disaster response, urban planning, environmental monitoring, and agricultural assessment. However, as with any advanced remote sensing analysis technology, there are potential dual-use risks, including unauthorized surveillance or potential privacy infringements. To mitigate these risks, our GeoVista framework and the APEX-GRO dataset are developed using publicly available, anonymized satellite and aerial imagery. We strongly advocate for the responsible, transparent, and regulated deployment of these technologies in strict compliance with international privacy standards and ethical guidelines. 22

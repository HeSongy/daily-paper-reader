We present an open-source visual analytics workbench for each of these steps. The system links embedding experiments to source data, metadata, spatial context, and model configurations, so latent-space results can be traced back to the physics. 1 INTRODUCTION 

Numerical weather prediction and Earth system models already produce petabyte-scale, multivariate, spatiotemporal datasets. AI-based weather and climate models compound this: they enable far larger ensembles and cheaper experimentation, opening the door to broader exploration of rare and extreme events. As dataset size grows and the field moves toward combining machine-learning methods with physics-based simulation, the bottleneck increasingly shifts from technical feasibility of producing forecasts to searching, interpreting, and validating the resulting data. Embedding-based representations offer a promising way to make these datasets searchable [3]. By mapping complex atmospheric states, spatial regions, or model outputs into vector spaces, em-beddings support similarity search, analog retrieval, clustering, and discovery of recurring patterns. This is attractive for researchers in Earth systems science, where there is often a need to find struc-turally related events across large archives or ensembles. How-ever, embedding-based search functionality might not be inher-ently scientifically meaningful. A nearest neighbor in latent space may reflect a physically relevant weather structure. It may also re-flect artifacts of preprocessing, geography, seasonality, model bias, or the particular representation used to generate the embeddings. This raises a trustworthiness challenge for scientific search: re-searchers need to determine whether retrieved neighbors are mean-ingful enough to support scientific reasoning, hypothesis genera-tion, or downstream analysis. Beyond a search interface, users must be able to inspect how embedding spaces are organized, compare alternative representations, formulate retrieval strategies, and verify results against physical meteorological evidence. In practice, these steps are difficult to combine. Embedding generation, dimensional-ity reduction, vector search, model auditing, geospatial inspection, and conventional meteorological visualization are often handled by separate scripts or tools. This fragmentation makes it hard to deter-mine which embedding models and query strategies are appropriate for a given scientific task. We present an open-source visual analytics workbench for devel-oping and auditing embedding-based search workflows for weather and climate data. The system links each embedding experiment to shared source data, metadata, spatial context, model configura-tion, and retrieval parameters, allowing users to inspect latent-space organization and verify retrieved neighbors against meteorological evidence. This paper makes three contributions: (a) A provenance-aware scientific retrieval . The workbench connects embedding vectors, source imagery, spatial coordinates, timestamps, metadata, and model configurations so that latent-space results can be traced back to physical data that produced it. (b) Interactive retrieval-strategy design for meteorological analog search . The system supports image-level, localized patch-level, metadata-constrained, spatially constrained, and global-to-local retrieval, enabling users to test whether nearest neighbors reflect meaningful atmospheric structures rather than representation artifacts. (c) A tropical-cyclone case study with scalable retrieval evaluation . We demonstrate the workflow using ECMWF Reanalysis v5 (ERA5) derived vision transformer (ViT) embeddings and International Best Track Archive for Climate Stewardship (IBTrACS) [9] storm metadata, and evaluate disk-backed vector search over tens of mil-lions of high-dimensional embeddings. The source code, example data, and instructions for the demon-strated workflows are released as supplemental material (Sec. 7) to support reproducibility and extension. 

2 BACKGROUND 

2.1 Big Data Challenges in Earth System Modeling 

Navigating petabyte-scale climate data was identified as a major challenge more than a decade ago [14] and remains a research pri-ority [20, 23]. Recent advances in AI-based Earth system modeling further intensify this challenge. AI weather prediction models now outperform operational numerical weather prediction systems on several metrics while requiring substantially fewer computational resources [2]. This also enables much larger ensembles [11], which are important for uncertainty quantification, rare-event analysis, and characterizing variability in chaotic atmospheric systems. As Earth system models increasingly combine machine learning with physical modeling [4], analysis workflows must support not only access to high-volume outputs, but also their interpretation, valida-tion, and search. Substantial work has addressed the technical challenge of ac-cessing and visualizing large Earth science datasets. Multi-resolution progressive access [16], cloud-native chunking strategies [1], and scientific visualization tools remain essential for moving from raw data to verifiable visual evidence. However, access and rendering alone do not solve the problem of deciding what to in-spect. Visual analytics systems address this gap by combining au-tomated analysis with interactive exploration. Keim et al. [8] argue for an “analyze first” workflow in which systems identify poten-tially important structure before users visually inspect details. Our work follows this tradition but focuses on embedding-based search as the automated analysis layer for weather and climate data, while preserving links to meteorological evidence for interpretation and validation. 

2.2 Embeddings, Search, and Latent Space Exploration Tools 

Representation learning is increasingly used to identify structure in Earth science data [10]. Climate research has a long history of vector-space representations, including empirical orthogonal func-tions based on principal component analysis [13]; modern embed-dings extend this idea using nonlinear architectures such as autoen-coders, contrastive models, and foundation models. Recent ex-amples include autoencoder-based ERA5 representations [24], Al-phaEarth Foundations and its embedding dataset for Earth observa-tion [3], analyses of Aurora model embeddings [19], and mechanis-tic interpretation tools for weather and climate model latent spaces [22]. Embedding-based search complements supervised event detec-tion methods such as ClimateNet [17] by enabling users to query for similar atmospheric states or localized structures without rely-ing only on predefined labels. This is important for discovery tasks involving rare events, hidden precursors, or “unknown unknowns,” where unsupervised and self-supervised methods can reduce depen-dence on expert labels [12]. Several recent systems address ad-jacent pieces of this problem. Embedding Atlas [18] provides a low-friction, general-purpose interface for inspecting large embed-ding projections through scalable rendering, density-based cluster-ing, and metadata cross-filtering, with the projection itself as the primary analysis surface. ClimateSOM [7] targets climate ensem-bles, using a self-organizing map and LLM-assisted sensemaking to compare variability across ensemble runs by laying members onto a learned 2D grid. Our workbench is designed for a different approach: embed-ding inspection and similarity retrieval are coupled stages of a sin-gle workflow. The system supports multiple coexisting embedding experiments over the same source data, with provenance back to the underlying meteorology, and the retrieval backend is built for out-of-core search over patch-level indexes that exceed workstation memory. The pace of representation learning also matters for sys-tem design. Different models, preprocessing choices, spatial scales, and similarity definitions yield different vector spaces and differ-ent retrieval behavior, and the right representation for a given sci-entific question is rarely known in advance. We therefore frame embedding-based retrieval as a visual analytics problem in which model comparison, search, interpretation, and scalable access are coupled requirements, and we organize the system around an em-bedding experiment abstraction that lets multiple representations of the same source data coexist and be evaluated against each other. 

3 DESIGN GOALS 

Embedding-based search is promising for exploring large weather and climate datasets, but it is difficult to use scientifically unless users can understand what is being searched, how similarity is de-fined and whether retrieved results correspond to meaningful phys-ical phenomena. Drawing on prior work and conversations with domain scientists, we designed the workbench around three goals: 

G1: Support provenance-aware embedding experiments. Each representation is treated as an experiment tied to a shared source dataset, capturing model configuration, preprocessing, spatial sam-pling, timestamps, metadata, and source imagery. This lets re-searchers load and compare representations from different archi-tectures; vision models, autoencoders, domain foundation models, latent spaces from AI weather emulators; while tracing any latent-space result back to the data and modeling choices that produced it. 

G2: Support interactive, domain-grounded retrieval design. 

What counts as meteorological similarity depends on spatial scale, metadata, geographic context, and the question being asked. Users should therefore be able to build and revise retrieval strategies; global-state, localized patch-level, metadata-constrained, spatially constrained, and staged global-to-local; and inspect results through familiar domain views: maps, timestamps, metadata, source im-agery, derived fields, and highlighted matching regions. Together these let users judge whether nearest neighbors reflect real atmo-spheric structure. 

G3: Support scalable out-of-core retrieval. Embedding work-flows can produce many vectors per timestep, ensemble member, variable, or region, quickly exceeding workstation memory. The system must support interactive retrieval at this scale without re-quiring all vectors in RAM or persistent access to high-memory HPC environments. This matters most during experimentation, when several embedding collections may coexist for the same dataset. 

4 SYSTEM DESIGN AND WORKFLOW 

The workbench is a reactive visual analytics application for explor-ing embedding-based search over weather and climate data. Its central abstraction is an embedding experiment: a set of vectors generated from a shared source reference using a particular model, preprocessing pipeline, spatial sampling scheme, and configuration (G1, Fig. 1(a)). This abstraction keeps the system model-agnostic. In the case study, we instantiate this design using image-level and patch-level embeddings from vision foundation models, but the same structure can represent embeddings from other architectures as long as they can be linked back to source data and metadata. The system separates source data from embedding experiments while preserving links between them (G2, Fig. 1(b)). Source ta-bles store the original or derived meteorological data, timestamps, spatial extent, and metadata. Experiment tables store model con-figuration, embedding vectors, spatial indexing information, and vector-search indexes. This separation allows multiple models or preprocessing choices to be evaluated over the same source dataset without duplicating the underlying data, while preserving the links needed to trace retrieval results from latent space back to their phys-ical origin. Users first load an experiment and inspect its associated source metadata, model configuration, embedding tables, index informa-tion, spatial extent, and available metadata fields. This inspec-tion step lets users verify which dataset, representation, and spa-tial sampling scheme they are analyzing before using the embed-ding space for search. Retrieval can be constrained by metadata or spatial filters, and the returned re-sults are linked back to source imagery, maps, timestamps, similar-ity scores, and highlighted matching regions when available. The retrieval backend uses disk-backed vector indexes through Lance [15] to support interactive search over large embedding col-lections (G3). This is important because localized representations can greatly increase the number of searchable vectors generated from each timestep, ensemble member, variable, or spatial region. The system is therefore designed to keep the visual workflow re-sponsive while allowing researchers to test retrieval strategies over millions of vectors. In Sec. 6, we evaluate this scalability. 

5 DEMONSTRATION : T ROPICAL -C YCLONE RETRIEVAL 

We demonstrate the workbench through a tropical-cyclone retrieval scenario over hourly ERA5 composites from the North Atlantic basin (2016–2018), paired with IBTrACS storm-track metadata. ERA5 variables are encoded as three-channel images of mean sea-level pressure anomaly, 10 m wind speed, and total column wa-ter vapor (Tab. 1; example composites in Fig. 2b–d). DINOv3 Base [21], a vision foundation model, produces image-level and patch-level embeddings. IBTrACS observations are spatiotempo-rally joined with each composite to provide storm labels and in-tensity metadata. The workflow described here illustrates the core retrieval loop; the full interaction sequence, including additional views and features omitted for space, is shown in the supplemental screen recording. The user first inspects the DINOv3 latent space through a UMAP projection colored by IBTrACS maximum sustained wind speed (Fig. 2a). Storm-containing composites form a distinct peripheral cluster; non-storm states remain broadly distributed. Box-selecting the cluster populates a linked gallery of raw composites, confirm-ing that the structure is meteorological rather than a latent-space artifact. This step grounds the embedding experiment as suitable for retrieval before any query is issued. The user then issues a localized query: a patch in Hurricane Matthew near the Florida coast (2016-10-08) with a spatial filter restricting matches to the Caribbean region around Puerto Rico (Fig. 1c). As expected, the nearest matches are Matthew’s own track frames followed by Hurricane Irma north of Puerto Rico (2017-09-08 to 2017-09-12) and Hurricane Maria during its landfall on the island (2017-09-15 to 2017-09-27). More distant matches include other 2017 and 2018 Atlantic systems crossing the filter region. Patch locations, timestamps, and similarity scores are ex-posed in the result gallery for export. We do not attempt to quantify the accuracy of the retrieved re-sults in this paper. Doing so meaningfully requires task-specific definitions of meteorological similarity, curated query sets, and in-terpretability of the embedding model - a study in its own right, and one for which the workbench is the supporting infrastructure rather than the object of evaluation. The workbench can isolate and export retrieval queries for exactly this kind of subsequent model-level benchmarking. This scenario illustrates how the workbench supports a practical discovery workflow: a known phenomenon is used to characterize a region of interest in the embedding space, and a constrained patch-level query then surfaces meteorologically similar events across the archive. The same query construction can be reused against em-bedding experiments built over other corpora: for example, AI-emulator ensembles or reanalysis archives to search for similar phe-nomena where storm-track labels are not readily available. 

6 SCALABILITY EVALUATION 

We benchmarked memory-mapped IVF-PQ indexes over patch-embedding corpora ranging from 0.98M to 23.55M 768-dimensional vectors. IVF-PQ combines an inverted file index (IVF)[5] , which partitions the vector space so that search can probe only a subset of candidate regions, with product quantization (PQ)[6], which compresses vectors to reduce storage and memory costs. Benchmarks were run on a HPC compute node configured with 16 GiB RAM and local NVMe SSD staging - simulating a commodity workstation. Each benchmark used exact nearest neigh-bors as ground truth. For each index, we ran 100 untimed warm-up queries followed by 1,000 timed queries, measuring mean latency for aggregate query cost, p95/p99 tail latency for worst-case inter-active behavior, peak process Resident Set Size (RSS) for memory use, and Recall@10 for approximate-search quality. RSS captures the process memory high-water mark, while Recall@10 measures agreement between approximate search results and the exact top-10 neighbors. Fig. 3 shows the backend supports out-of-core search well beyond available memory. 7 CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 

We propose an Earth system data search and discovery workbench powered by vector embeddings, to address the growing challenge of exploring large, high-dimensional weather and climate datasets. Recognizing the diversity of embedding models and retrieval strate-gies, as well as the rapid pace of development in representation learning, we designed the system as an extensible environment for experimentation rather than a single-purpose search interface. This short paper demonstrates the first steps toward this goal: organizing embedding experiments around shared source data, supporting in-spectable search workflows, and evaluating the technical feasibility of out-of-core retrieval over large embedding collections. Building on ongoing collaboration with domain scientists, future work needs to refine the user experience for query formulation, re-trieval interpretation, and integration with existing XAI workflows. Beyond qualitative visual verification of retrieved results, the work-bench can isolate and export retrieval queries for subsequent bench-marking of embedding models. Such model-level evaluation is a study in its own right, requiring task-specific definitions of mete-orological similarity, carefully designed query sets, appropriate re-trieval strategies, and ground-truth labels. The current implemen-tation supports image-level and patch-level similarity search using vision models; future work will explore richer query constructions, including vector combinations, vectors for segmented spatial re-gions, and multi-region queries. Finally, embeddings provide a natural bridge to multi-modal and language-driven interfaces for Earth system data. As large lan-guage and vision-language models become increasingly integrated with scientific workflows, future versions of the workbench could explore richer query specification and cross-modal reasoning over data, metadata, and literature.

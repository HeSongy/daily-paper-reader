## Abstract 

Multi-turn jailbreak attacks progressively erode LLM safety alignment across seemingly innocuous conversation turns, achieving success rates exceeding 90% against state-of-the-art models. Existing alignment-based and guardrail methods suffer from three key limitations: they require costly weight modification, evaluate each turn independently without modeling cumulative safety erosion, and detect attacks only after harmful content has been generated. To address these limitations, we first formulate the 

proactive early jailbreak detection problem with a new metric, detection lead ,that measures how early an attack can be detected before the LLM com-plies. We then propose S AFE DREAM , a lightweight world-model-based framework that operates as an external module without modifying the LLM’s weights. S AFE DREAM introduces three components: (1) a safety state world model that encodes LLM hidden states into a compact safety representation and predicts how it evolves across turns, (2) CUSUM de-tection that accumulates weak per-turn risk signals into reliable evidence, and (3) contrastive imagination that simultaneously rolls out attack and be-nign futures in latent space to issue early alarms before jailbreaks occur. On three multi-turn jailbreak benchmarks (XGuard-Train, SafeDialBench, SafeMTData) against 8 baselines, S AFE DREAM achieves the best detection timeliness across all benchmarks (1.06–1.20 turns before compliance) while maintaining competitive false positive rates and outperforming baselines in detection quality. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) (Yang et al., 2024) can be exploited for harmful purposes such as generating misinformation or illegal instructions. Despite safety mechanisms like alignment training and guardrails (Inan et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024), multi-turn jailbreak attacks remain highly effective (Mazeika et al., 2024): by spreading malicious intent across seemingly innocuous turns, they gradually erode LLM safety barriers until the model complies. Crescendo (Russinovich et al., 2025) achieves a 98% binary success rate against GPT-4, FITD (Weng et al., 2025) averages 94% Attack Success Rate (ASR) across seven LLMs, and STAR (Li et al., 2026) reaches 94.5% Safety Failure Rate (SFR) against GPT-4o. Recent work has explored various approaches to counter jailbreak attempts. Alignment-based methods such as Circuit Breaker (Zou et al., 2024), X-Boundary (Lu et al., 2025), and TARS (Kim et al., 2025) modify LLM weights to resist attacks. Guardrail methods such as WildGuard (Han et al., 2024) and GuardReasoner (Liu et al., 2025) deploy external classifiers to evaluate each turn independently. ## 2 Problem Formulation 

Multi-turn dialogue and safety model. We study jailbreak detection given an ongoing dialogue between a user and an LLM, the goal is to decide whether the user is steering the conversation toward harmful content before the LLM complies. At turn t, the user sends utterance ut and the LLM generates response yt. We define the observation ot =(u1, y1, . . . , yt−1, ut) as the conversation prefix immediately before yt is generated. A safety model f (ot) ∈ { 0, 1 } infers whether jailbreak intent is present; if f (ot) = 1, the LLM refuses to respond. 

Attack model and compliance point. In a multi-turn jailbreak, an adversarial user pro-gressively guides the LLM toward harmful content through seemingly innocuous messages, gradually eroding the LLM’s safety alignment until the model fully complies. We call this critical point the compliance point c, formally defined as the first turn at which the majority of independent safety classifiers flag the LLM’s response as harmful (see Section 3.4 for the labeling procedure). Detecting the attack after the compliance point ( t > c) is too late, as harmful content has been generated. 

Proactive early jailbreak detection. Previous defenses show little concern for how early 

an attack can be detected: guardrails (Han et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025) flag harmful content only after it appears, and alignment-based methods (Zou et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2025) resist attacks but do not anticipate compliance. We formulate the proactive early jailbreak detection problem: the safety model f predicts whether the dialogue will eventually reach a compliance point, and we measure timeliness via the detection lead :Lead = c − tdet , where tdet = min {t : f (ot) = 1}. Positive Lead ( tdet < c) achieves proactive early detection; negative Lead indicates reactive post-hoc detection. The objective is to maximize Lead while maintaining a low false positive rate on benign conversations. 

## 3 SafeDream: Proactive World Model for Early Jailbreak Detection 

We present S AFE DREAM , a world-model-based framework for proactive early jailbreak detection (Fig. 2). The framework introduces a safety state world model (Section 3.1) that encodes the LLM’s hidden state into compact latent safety states and learns to predict future safety states. To reliably detect jailbreak, we employ cumulative sum detection (Section 3.2), which aggregates weak per-turn risk signals into robust statistical evidence. Building on the world model and cumulative detection, we propose contrastive imagination (Section 3.3), which simultaneously predicts attack and benign future roll-outs to quantify vulnerability, enabling proactive early attack detection. We describe the training procedure in Section 3.4. 

3.1 Safety State World Model 

Proactive early detection requires the ability to predict future safety outcomes from the current state. To this end, we design a world model over safety states, consisting of two components: (1) a safety state extractor eϕ that compresses each turn into a low-dimensional safety state vector zt = eϕ(ot), capturing the safety-relevant information of that turn; and (2) a transition model gθ that integrates safety states from previous turns and predicts future safety states based on user actions at = ψ(ut). Each safety state is converted to risk score by a discriminator dω (zt) → rt ∈ [0, 1 ], where a value of 1 indicates likely jailbreak compliance. 

Safety state extraction. At each turn t, we feed the conversation prefix ot through the frozen Qwen2.5-7B (Yang et al., 2024) and extract the hidden state ht ∈ R3584 from layer 19, which Wollschl ¨ager et al. (2025) identify as the layer most strongly associated with safety-related refusal behavior in Qwen-family models. To extract safety-relevant informa-tion, we project ht onto K=5 safety-related directions on the concept cone (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025), yielding a safety signature st ∈ R5. We further enrich st via Perceiver-style cross-attention (Jaegle et al., 2022) that uses st as queries into ht, producing a learned 64-dimensional extension. The final safety state is: 

zt = [ st ; XAttn ϕ(st, ht)] ∈ R69 , (2) where the first 5 dimensions are frozen cone projections and the remaining 64 are learned enhancement through ϕ. Architectural details are in Appendix A.2. 

Transition model. The user action at = ψ(ut) is encoded by embedding the utterance ut

with the frozen LLM, mean-pooling over token embeddings, and linearly projecting the result to a 64-dimensional vector. A causal Transformer gθ then models the safety-state dynamics (Hafner et al., 2025). The goal of the transition model is to predict the next safety state from the current state and user action: given the input token [zt−1; at], the model outputs the predicted ˆ zt:ˆzt = gθ

Safety discriminator. A two-layer MLP dω (zt) → rt ∈ [0, 1 ] maps each safety state to a risk score, which is used by the cumulative detection module (Section 3.2) and the contrastive imagination module (Section 3.3).

3.3 Contrastive Imagination for Early Attack Detection 

When CUSUM enters the gray zone, instead of waiting for more turns, S AFE DREAM lever-ages the world model (Section 3.1) to imagine what would happen next. We first construct two action pools from the training data: an attack pool Aatk containing encoded attack utterances and a benign pool Aben containing encoded benign utterances. For each condi-tion γ ∈ { atk, ben }, we randomly sample actions from the corresponding pool to simulate different futures. Specifically, we roll out M=8 trajectories of H=3 steps from the current state, autoregressively sampling actions, predicting states via gθ , scoring via dω, and accu-mulating the imagined CUSUM (full procedure in Appendix A.3). ## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Datasets. We evaluate on three multi-turn jailbreak benchmarks that cover complementary attack characteristics. XGuard-Train (Rahman et al., 2025) (30K conversations, 13 risk categories, adaptive multi-agent attacks) serves as our primary benchmark due to its scale and diversity. SafeDialBench (Cao et al., 2025) (4K conversations, 7 distinct attack strategies, bilingual) tests generalization across diverse attack tactics. SafeMTData (Ren et al., 2025) (1.7K conversations, actor-network attacks, ∼8.7 turns) provides backward compatibility with prior work such as TARS. For each benchmark, to prevent the model from learning the topic shortcuts for detection, we construct topic-matched benign contrasts to force models to distinguish based on trajectory dynamics rather than topic.

4.3 Ablation Studies 

To understand the contribution of each component, we systematically remove one module at a time and measure the impact on detection quality and timeliness (Table 2). • Imagination enables early detection. Removing imagination (Section 3.3) drops Lead from 1.11 to 0.53, confirming that imagination is the key of proactive early detection. • CUSUM enables low false alarm rate. Removing CUSUM (Section 3.2) increases Lead to 1.49, however, FPR surges to 13.8%, indicating that CUSUM is essential for suppressing false alarms. • Concept cone grounds the latent space. Removing the concept cone (Section 3.1) causes the most severe overall degradation (F1: 0.891 →0.740, FPR: 7.9% →15.5%), indicating that the cone projection encodes essential safety-relevant knowledge. • Transition model enables dynamics. Removing the transition model (Section 3.1) drops Lead to 0.30 and F1 to 0.775, as the system loses both imagination capability and multi-turn state integration and reduces to single-turn classification. • Contrastive design calibrates imagination. Attack-only imagination (Section 3.3) re-duces Lead from 1.11 to 1.01 and increases FPR from 7.9% to 25.6%, confirming that comparing against benign futures is necessary to avoid overestimating vulnerability. We further sweep the imagination hyperparameters ( H, M) and concept cone dimensionality (K). Fig. 4 shows that H=3 and M=8 maximize Lead, with diminishing returns beyond these values, while the full 5-dim cone consistently outperforms lower-dimensional projections. Additional ablations (transition model architecture) are in Appendix C.3.

Most critically, both paradigms are reactive : they detect harmful content only after it manifests. Safety representations and world models. Arditi et al. (2024) discover a refusal direction in LLM hidden states; RDO (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025) extends this to a multi-dimensional concept cone; SRR (Du et al., 2025) and ReGA (Wei et al., 2025) leverage internal repre-sentations for safety ranking and runtime safeguarding. S AFE DREAM shares with these methods the insight that LLM hidden states encode rich safety-relevant geometric structure. However, prior work treats these representations as static features for single-turn classifi-cation or analysis, without modeling how they evolve during a multi-turn conversation. SAFE DREAM addresses this gap by learning a transition model over safety states, enabling prediction of future safety trajectories. This dynamics-based approach draws on world models from RL (Hafner et al., 2025; Teoh et al., 2025), which learn environment dynamics and use imagined rollouts to evaluate futures without real interaction. While world models are typically used for planning (selecting optimal actions), S AFE DREAM adapts them for 

detection : imagining both attack and benign futures to assess whether a conversation is heading toward jailbreak compliance, thus achieves proactive warning with low FPR. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We proposed S AFE DREAM , a lightweight external module that requires no LLM weight modification. S AFE DREAM formulates the proactive early jailbreak detection problem and introduces a new metric, detection lead , to measure detection timeliness. It combines a safety state world model for future state prediction, CUSUM for multi-turn evidence accumulation, and contrastive imagination for proactive early warning. On three benchmarks against 8 baselines, S AFE DREAM achieves the best detection timeliness (1.06–1.20 turns before compliance) while maintaining competitive false positive rates, outperforming all baselines in detection quality. Ablation studies confirm that each component is effective: contrastive imagination drives early detection, CUSUM suppresses false alarms, and the concept cone grounds the safety latent space. Limitations are discussed in Appendix D.

A.2 Architecture Details Why Transformer over RNN. Multi-turn attack strategies involve long-range dependen-cies: gradual escalation, strategy switching, and topic drift span many turns. The self-attention mechanism naturally captures dependencies between arbitrary turn pairs. Teoh et al. (2025) confirm that Transformers can learn compact latent world models effectively. 

Layer selection. We select layer 19 by running the Refusal Direction Optimization (RDO) algorithm (Wollschl ¨ager et al., 2025) on harmful and harmless prompts from SALAD-Bench (Li et al., 2024), sweeping over layers and selecting the one that yields the highest refusal manipulation effectiveness for Qwen2.5-7B. This layer choice is model- and scale-specific and must be re-determined for other architectures. 

Cross-attention details. The safety signature st ∈ R5 (detached from the computation graph) is linearly projected to produce query vectors Q = WQst. The full hidden state 

ht ∈ R3584 is partitioned into a sequence of fixed-size chunks, and each chunk is linearly projected to produce keys K and values V. Standard cross-attention retrieves the most relevant features: XAttn ϕ(st, ht) = softmax (QK ⊤/√dk)V ∈ Rdext .

Transition model details. The Transformer gθ has 4 layers, 4 attention heads, model dimension 128, and Pre-LN normalization. Since zt ∈ R69 and at ∈ R64 are concatenated, a linear layer projects the 133-dimensional input to the internal dimension of 128; output tokens are projected back to 69 dimensions. 12 Preprint. A.3 Imagination Procedure Details Action pool construction. The attack pool Aatk and benign pool Aben are constructed from the training fold of each cross-validation split. We encode every user turn from attack conversations as at = ψ(ut) and collect them into Aatk ; likewise, all user turns from benign conversations form Aben . ## B Full Experimental Setup 

B.1 Dataset Statistics 

We use XGuard-Train (Rahman et al., 2025) as our primary evaluation dataset. XGuard-Train contains 30,695 multi-turn jailbreak conversations spanning 13 risk categories, generated via the X-Teaming multi-agent framework with diverse attack strategies. The average dialogue length is around 5 turns. 

Topic-matched benign construction. For each attack dialogue, we extract the topic from the first user turn and prompt GPT-4o to generate a multi-turn benign conversation on the same topic with a similar number of turns. To ensure quality, we verify with all three labeling classifiers (HarmBench, GPT-4o, MD-Judge) that no turn in the generated benign conversation is flagged as harmful; any flagged conversation is regenerated. This topic-matching procedure ensures that the only distinguishing signal between attack and benign conversations is the trajectory dynamics (gradual safety erosion), not the surface-level topic. We apply the same procedure to all three benchmarks (XGuard-Train, SafeDialBench, SafeMTData). 

B.2 Baseline Descriptions 

We compare against two categories of baselines: 

Guardrail methods (5): WildGuard (Han et al., 2024), R2-Guard (Kang & Li, 2025), Bin-goGuard (Yin et al., 2025), GuardReasoner (Liu et al., 2025), and SelfDefend (Wang et al., 2025). 

Alignment-based methods (3): Circuit Breaker (Zou et al., 2024), X-Boundary (Lu et al., 2025), and TARS (Kim et al., 2025). 

Detection protocol for alignment-based methods. Alignment-based methods (Circuit Breaker, X-Boundary, TARS) modify LLM weights to resist attacks rather than produc-ing explicit detection signals. To evaluate them as detectors under our unified protocol, we define detection as follows: at each turn t, we feed the conversation prefix ot to the alignment-modified LLM and check whether its response constitutes a refusal (using the refusal detection task of WildGuard (Han et al., 2024)). If the alignment-modified LLM refuses at turn t, we record t as the detection turn tdet . If the model complies (produces non-refusal content), the turn is not flagged. For computing a continuous risk score (needed for AUROC/AUPRC), we use the refusal probability from the alignment-modified LLM’s output logits where available, or the WildGuard refusal confidence score otherwise. Note that this protocol gives alignment-based methods a natural advantage on FPR: because they directly modify the model’s refusal behavior, they rarely refuse benign conversations, yielding inherently low false positive rates. This explains why TARS achieves the lowest FPR in our evaluation despite being designed for safety alignment rather than detection. 

Detection protocol for guardrail methods. Each guardrail evaluates the user prompt and/or LLM response at every turn independently. We use each guardrail’s default prompt template and harmfulness task configuration. A turn is flagged as harmful if the guardrail’s output exceeds its default decision threshold. The detection turn tdet is the first flagged turn in the conversation. For AUROC/AUPRC computation, we use each guardrail’s continuous confidence score (probability or logit) as the per-conversation risk score, taking the maximum across all turns. Since guardrails evaluate each turn independently without modeling multi-turn dynamics, they can only detect attacks after harmful content manifests, resulting in negative Lead values. 

Guardrail model versions. WildGuard: allenai/wildguard (7B, response harmfulness task). R2-Guard: official implementation with MLN reasoning (Kang & Li, 2025). Bin-goGuard: llama3.1-8B variant, 5-level severity grading (levels ≥3 counted as harmful) (Yin et al., 2025). GuardReasoner: 8B model with chain-of-thought reasoning enabled (Liu 14 Preprint. Under review. et al., 2025). SelfDefend: fine-tuned Llama-2-7B with dual-prompt pipeline (P direct + P intent) (Wang et al., 2025). 

Alignment-based model training on Qwen2.5-7B. All alignment-based methods were retrained on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct following each method’s official training procedure and publicly released code: Circuit Breaker uses representation rerouting with the retain set from Zou et al. (2024) adapted to Qwen’s layer structure. X-Boundary follows the official code release with both single-turn and multi-turn safety training data (Lu et al., 2025). TARS uses the λ=0.5 harmful/harmless reward mixture with GRPO on Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct (Kim et al., 2025). 

Labeling classifier details. Turn-level labels are produced by HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) (fine-tuned Llama-2-13B classifier), GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024) ( gpt-4o-2024-08-06 ), and MD-Judge (Li et al., 2024) (fine-tuned Mistral-7B). Each classifier receives only the LLM’s response text for the current turn (without conversation history) to ensure per-turn independence.

Compliance point and label design. Defining what constitutes a “successful” jailbreak— and at which turn it occurs—is a recognized open problem. Ran et al. (2024) document that “there is surprisingly no consensus on how to evaluate whether a jailbreak attempt is success-ful.” Existing approaches span a wide spectrum: conversation-level binary (Crescendo (Russi-novich et al., 2025): at least one turn flagged by a judge), final-turn outcome (TROJail: reward on last response only), per-turn binary (HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024): classify each response independently), and multi-turn escalation (Siege (Zhou, 2025): tree-search-based multi-turn attacks that exploit partial compliance accumulating across turns). Siege demonstrates that minor concessions can be systematically exploited to eventually obtain disallowed content. We adopt a unified labeling framework based on a consensus of three safety classifiers— HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024), GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024), and MD-Judge (Li et al., 2024)—each evaluating every response independently. The graduated label averages their outputs and serves both as the training signal for the safety discriminator and as the basis for the compliance point definition: • Training : the graduated labels ℓt capture progressive safety degradation, providing the discriminator with a smooth signal that reflects escalation dynamics rather than a sharp binary transition. • Evaluation : the compliance point c is defined as the first turn at which ℓt ≥ 0.67 (a majority of classifiers flag the response as harmful). Detection Lead is then Lead = c − tdet . Using the same threshold for training and evaluation ensures consistency: the model is trained to detect turns that the evaluation deems harmful. This unified design ensures that training and evaluation operate on the same notion of safety degradation. ## D Limitations 

SAFE DREAM requires access to the frozen LLM’s intermediate representations (layer 19 hidden states), limiting deployment to settings where hidden states are accessible. The concept cone geometry is model-family-specific and must be re-extracted for each target LLM; validating the anchoring across diverse model families remains future work. The transition model learns dynamics from specific attack strategies observed during training; zero-shot transfer to entirely unseen attack families may require retraining. The contrastive imagination samples actions i.i.d. per step from the action pools, while real attacks may exhibit strategic correlations that the i.i.d. assumption could underestimate. Additionally, our evaluation relies on three specific safety classifiers to define ground-truth compliance points; disagreements among classifiers or blind spots shared by all three could introduce systematic labeling bias. Finally, while we evaluate on three diverse benchmarks, all experiments use a single target LLM (Qwen2.5-7B); extending to other model families (e.g., Llama, Mistral) requires validating and re-extracting the concept cone geometry and retraining the transition model, though the lightweight architecture makes this practical. 18

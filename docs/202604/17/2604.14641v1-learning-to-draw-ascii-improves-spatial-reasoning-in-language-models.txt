# Learning to Draw ASCII Improves Spatial Reasoning in Language Models 

Shiyuan Huang *, Li Liu *, Jincheng He, Leilani H. Gilpin †

University of California, Santa Cruz {shuan101, lliu112, jhe516, lgilpin}@ucsc.edu 

Abstract 

When faced with complex spatial problems, humans naturally sketch layouts to organize their thinking, and the act of drawing further sharpens their understanding. In this work, we ask whether a similar principle holds for Large Language Models (LLMs): can learning to con-struct explicit visual layouts from spatial de-scriptions instill genuine spatial understanding? We introduce T EXT 2S PACE , a dataset that pairs natural language descriptions with ground-truth ASCII grid layouts and spatial QA pairs, en-abling us to separate failures in constructing spatial representations from failures in reason-ing over them. We adopt ASCII because it is human-readable, operates entirely within the token space of language models, and encodes spatial relations in a structurally verifiable form. Our evaluation reveals a pronounced “Read-Write Asymmetry”: LLMs interpret ASCII rep-resentations effectively but struggle to produce them from text, and these construction errors propagate to incorrect answers downstream. To address this limitation, we train models on lay-out construction (Text →ASCII) and find that it significantly improves spatial reasoning from text alone, even without producing any ASCII at inference time. Combining construction with comprehension training further amplifies these gains. Crucially, these improvements transfer to three external spatial reasoning benchmarks, demonstrating that, much as sketching sharpens human spatial thinking, learning to construct explicit layouts instills spatial understanding that generalizes beyond the training format. 

1 Introduction 

Spatial reasoning (Byrne and Johnson-Laird, 1989; Newcombe and Huttenlocher, 2000) is fundamen-tal to human cognition (Malanchini et al., 2020). As large language models (LLMs) are increasingly

However, existing benchmarks are limited to evaluating final answers (Yamada et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). When a model fails, it is unclear whether the error stems from a flawed spatial rep-resentation or from faulty reasoning over a cor-rect one. More critically, even correct answers may reflect surface-level pattern matching rather than robust spatial understanding. A model can ex-ploit lexical cues in simple configurations yet fail unpredictably as spatial complexity grows. Prior work on faithful reasoning has shown that correct answers produced through unreliable intermedi-ate steps offer no guarantee of consistent behavior on novel problems (Jacovi and Goldberg, 2020; Creswell and Shanahan, 2022; Parcalabescu and Frank, 2024; Xiong et al., 2025). Without a way to verify the model’s intermediate spatial representa-tion, these failure modes remain invisible. To open this black box, we use ASCII grid lay-outs as an explicit, structured text that makes the reasoning process itself observable. The ASCII representation is well suited as this diagnostic lens because it lives within the model’s own token space, is immediately interpretable by humans, and can be formally verified against ground truth. We intro-duce T EXT 2S PACE , a benchmark of diverse spatial configurations paired with natural language descrip-tions, images, and three styles of ASCII grids. Each instance includes a ground-truth layout, providing verifiable supervision for both diagnosing represen-tation failures and training models toward robust spatial understanding. Training models on layout construction improves spatial reasoning from text alone, even without producing any ASCII at inference time. Compre-hension training alone does not consistently im-prove performance, but it leads to gains when com-bined with construction, producing the strongest overall results. These gains transfer to three ex-ternal benchmarks, confirming that construction training builds transferable spatial understanding rather than format-specific heuristics. We present three contributions: (1) a benchmark of spatial configurations paired with ground-truth ASCII layouts, enabling evaluation that separates representation failures from reasoning failures; (2) identification of the construction gap , showing that models comprehend spatial layouts more reliably than they construct them and that this asymmetry propagates into downstream reasoning; and (3) em-pirical evidence that learning to construct spatial layouts fosters transferable internal spatial under-standing, validated on three external benchmarks. 

2 Related Work 

Linguistic Spatial Reasoning Benchmarks. 

Early work on spatial language processing focused on extracting formal representations from natural language text (Kordjamshidi et al., 2011). Subse-quent benchmarks like bAbI (Weston et al., 2015) introduced positional reasoning, while S TEP GAME 

(Shi et al., 2022) and S PART QA (Mirzaee et al., 2021) advanced the field with more complicated reasoning and broader linguistic coverage. How-ever, a critical limitation remains across these benchmarks: they rely exclusively on final-answer accuracy. This "black-box" evaluation obscures the underlying inference process, making it impossible to distinguish between failures in representation and failures in reasoning. Our work addresses this methodological gap by introducing ground-truth intermediate representations, enabling precise di-agnosis of spatial reasoning errors. While integrating visual modalities (VLMs) of-2fers a potential remedy for spatial understanding, recent studies reveal that VLMs face their own chal-lenges in visual perception (Liu et al., 2023, 2024; Song et al., 2024; Kamath et al., 2023) and spatial reasoning (Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025a; Ogezi and Shi, 2025). Rather than relying on a separate visual encoder, we use ASCII grids to rep-resent spatial layouts entirely within the language model’s native token space. These text-native grids serve a dual purpose: they act not only as a reason-ing source within the context but also as a verifiable representation of all spatial relations. 

ASCII and Spatial Representation. The utility of intermediate reasoning steps is well-established through Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) and its extensions, such as Tree-of-Thoughts (Yao et al., 2023) and latent reasoning (Hao et al., 2024). Beyond purely linguistic traces, prior work has shown that symbolic, text-native spa-tial representations can effectively support spatial reasoning in language models, including grid-based worlds and discrete relational layouts (Côté et al., 2018). Visualization-of-Thought (Wu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025) draws on human mental imagery to generate symbolic or pixel-level visualizations for spatial reasoning. While we share the intuition that explicit spatial construction aids reasoning, we focus on a different question: when and why does this process fail, and can training on it improve per-formance? We use ASCII as a diagnostic medium because, unlike natural language, its spatial outputs are structurally verifiable against ground truth. 

Generalization of Spatial Reasoning. Recent research demonstrates that enhancing specific ca-pabilities in LLMs often yields transfer benefits to other domains. For instance, Shao et al. (2024) indi-cate that training on mathematics improves general reasoning benchmarks, not just quantitative tasks. Prior work shows that spatial training benefits vi-sual reasoning tasks (Wei et al., 2025; Han et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2025), typically through training on perception or comprehension objectives. We study a complementary mechanism: whether learning to construct spatial layouts can strengthen spatial understanding in language mod-els, yielding transferable gains in spatial reasoning.

3.1 Data Generation 

The dataset is constructed from connected spatial graphs where entities are linked via eight direc-tional relationships: above, below, left, right, upper-left, upper-right, lower-left, lower-right . All rela-tions adhere to a unified coordinate system, serving as the ground truth for both ASCII diagrams and rendered images. Instances are generated using a breadth-first search (BFS) procedure to guarantee connectivity. We enforce strict validation rules: self-loops and duplicate edges are prohibited, and any configuration conflicting with existing spatial constraints is rejected. These safeguards ensure every instance represents a valid, internally consis-tent topology. A detailed data generation process is presented in the Appendix F. We vary complexity from simple two-component structures to intricate graphs with up to eight com-ponents and twelve relations, capturing configura-tions that require transitive reasoning across multi-ple inference steps. Each instance includes natural language descriptions synthesized from the spatial graph, using diverse synonyms to ensure linguis-tic variation. Corresponding queries are designed so that target object pairs admit unique inferability, guaranteeing a single unambiguous spatial relation. 33.2 Multi-Format Representations ASCII Visualization: We include three rendering styles to test robustness against formatting varia-tions. The simple format uses minimal spacing without borders for clarity. The grid format intro-duces structure using +, -, and | characters. The 

panel format employs box-drawing characters for a visually distinct layout. Image Representation: 

The spatial arrangement is also rendered in a stan-dardized image format with configurable grid lines and directional arrows, serving as both a multi-modal evaluation target and a human-readable ref-erence. Further dataset details and data examples are provided in Appendix A. 

4 Experiments 

We split T EXT 2S PACE into training and test sets, with the test set consisting of 1,000 samples used in the following experiments. We choose the ASCII 

grid format throughout our experiments; details of this format are provided in Appendix B. Our experiments address three questions in sequence. 

Benchmarking: Can models both read and draw spatial layouts, and where does the gap lie? Rea-soning process: When models sketch a layout as an intermediate step, does the quality of that sketch help or hurt downstream reasoning? Generaliza-tion: Does training on layout construction instill spatial understanding that transfers to text-only rea-soning and external benchmarks, even when no layout is produced at inference time?

4.2 Task Setups 

Since ASCII is a unique representation that en-codes two-dimensional spatial structure within a one-dimensional token sequence, we first evalu-ate language models’ ability to understand and generate ASCII representations. Specifically, we assess two complementary capabilities: generat-ing an ASCII representation from a textual de-scription ( Desc →ASCII ) and generating a natural-language description from an ASCII representation (ASCII →Desc ). We then evaluate spatial reasoning tasks, de-noted R, in which the model is given a reason-ing source and asked to answer queries about spa-tial relationships. RDesc refers to reasoning from textual descriptions alone, while RASCII refers to reasoning from ASCII representations alone. Be-yond reasoning from a single source, we investigate the role of ASCII as an intermediate step along-side descriptions through two generation orders. In 

RDesc:(Ans, ASCII) , the model first produces the an-swer and then generates an ASCII representation. In RDesc:(ASCII, Ans) , the model first generates an ASCII representation and then uses it to answer the query. For both orders, we consider one-turn generation , where the model produces both out-puts within a single response, and two-turn gen-eration , where the second output is elicited via a follow-up prompt. The two generation orders mir-ror established self-explanation paradigms (Huang et al., 2023; Madsen et al., 2024): we compare two generation orders: predict-then-explain versus explain-then-predict (Camburu et al., 2018). Finally, we evaluate RDesc+ASCII . In this phase, we substitute the model-generated ASCII with the ground-truth ASCII before the model answers the query. This replacement strategy simulates an ideal reasoning process, allowing us to assess the upper-bound performance of the model when guided by an ideal spatial representation. 

4.3 Evaluation Methods 

Our evaluation protocol targets three key aspects of the model’s performance: QA Correctness: 

For the spatial reasoning QA tasks, we validate the prediction against a predefined set of rela-tion aliases. Representation Alignment: For the 

ASCII ↔Desc conversion tasks, we measure the correspondence between converted representations. For ASCII →Desc , the ASCII diagram is treated as the reference. We evaluate, across all generated descriptions, the percentage that can be correctly inferred from the given ASCII representation. For 

Desc →ASCII , we evaluate the generated ASCII di-agrams, RDesc:(Ans, ASCII) and RDesc:(ASCII, Ans) , by taking the description as the reference and mea-suring the proportion of the description that is cor-rectly reflected in the ASCII output. Reasoning Consistency: For the joint generation settings, we 4introduce two complementary metrics to analyze internal coherence. Consistency(Cons): Measures the alignment between the generated ASCII and the generated Answer. This determines if the model’s intermediate output supports its final prediction. Consistency Accuracy(C_Acc): Measures answer accuracy strictly on instances where the ASCII and Answer are consistent. By controlling for internal coherence, this isolates the model’s reasoning capa-bility, helping distinguish whether errors arise from failures in constructing spatial representations or from limitations in reasoning over them. 

4.4 Learning Spatial Relations with ASCII 

We propose that ASCII layouts function as a struc-tured reasoning intermediate: a verifiable “cogni-tive map” that makes spatial relationships explicit. We hypothesize that learning to construct such maps (Lightman et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2025b) consolidates spatial logic during training. If so, models trained on layout construction should im-prove on spatial reasoning tasks even without pro-ducing any layout at inference time, and the benefit should transfer beyond the training format. To test and disentangle the understanding and generation of ASCII representations, we design three config-urations. Learning to Construct (Desc →ASCII )forces the model to actively construct spatial lay-outs from text (performing “mental mapping”). 

Learning to Comprehend (ASCII →Desc ) focuses on parsing and verbalizing ASCII representations. 

Bidirectional Alignment (Desc ↔ASCII ) com-bines both tasks simultaneously. We fine-tuned QWEN 3-30B-A3B on 4x NVIDIA A40 GPUs using the SFTTrainer from the HuggingFace TRL library (von Werra et al., 2020). We employed Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021; Mangrulkar et al., 2022) for parameter-efficient training, utilizing a split of 4,000 training and 500 validation examples. We adhered to the default library configurations for all other settings; full training details are provided in Appendix C. We evaluate performance on the Spatial Rea-soning Task ( RDesc ), where models answer spatial relations solely from natural language descriptions without explicit ASCII input. Spatial Reasoning Performance Table 2 reports accuracy on spatial reasoning tasks ( R) under dif-ferent settings. On the text-only baseline ( RDesc ), accuracy ranges from 42.0% (Q WEN 2.5-7B) to 93.3% ( GPT -OSS -20B). This spread confirms that our benchmark meaningfully differentiates spatial reasoning ability, yet most models have substan-tial room to improve. This motivates examining whether ASCII representations can deepen spatial understanding and narrow this gap. Providing ASCII as input substantially refines performance. Most models reason more accu-rately from ASCII alone ( RASCII ) than from text descriptions ( RDesc ), and the combination of both (RDesc+ASCII ) yields the highest accuracy. This shows that ASCII serves as a critical signal for resolving spatial ambiguities in natural language. In contrast, model-generated ASCII as an in-termediate step before answering does not im-prove reasoning. Answering first and then drawing (RDesc(Ans, ASCII) ) roughly matches the text-only baseline ( RDesc ), while drawing first and then an-swering ( RDesc(ASCII, Ans) ) drops below it across all base models in both one-turn and two-turn settings. With generated ASCII accuracy at only 12-50% across models, the sketches are not accurate enough to serve as a promising reasoning scaffold. Since provided ASCII yields the highest accuracy while model-generated ASCII degrades it, the bottleneck is sketch quality, not the format itself. This echoes prior findings that generating intermediate artifacts can reduce task accuracy (Camburu et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2023). However, unlike unstructured self-explanations or textual CoT (Wei et al., 2022), where intermediate errors are difficult to detect, ASCII is structurally verifiable. This allows us to directly trace the degradation to flawed construc-tion rather than treating it as a black-box effect. Models can reason over spatial layouts when pro-vided, but cannot yet produce them reliably enough to support their own reasoning. This diagnosis points to a concrete path forward: since the deficiency lies in representation construc-tion, the next step is to examine whether training on this capability can strengthen the model’s spa-tial understanding. We first analyze whether the model’s generated layouts are internally coherent with its answers, using consistency as an unsuper-vised signal of reasoning reliability (Section 5.2). We then investigate whether training on layout construction can instill the spatial understanding needed to bridge this discrepancy (Section 5.3). 

5.2 Consistency Analysis in Spatial Reasoning 

Table 3 shows the alignment between ASCII rep-resentations and textual answers across both out-6put orders and turn settings. We report three key metrics: (1) Answer Accuracy measures overall task performance; (2) Consistency quantifies the alignment between the generated ASCII and the textual answer; and (3) Consistent Accuracy evalu-ates correctness specifically on instances where the representation and answer correspond. In the one-turn generation setting, producing the ASCII representation before the answer does not improve consistency. Similar consistency across output orders suggests that the model does not clearly use the ASCII as a reasoning scaffold, with both outputs likely reflecting the same underlying representation. In the two-turn generation setting, we observed an improved consistency rate, where the model first generates either the answer or the ASCII, and is then prompted to produce the other. The consistency gap between RDesc:(Ans, ASCII) and 

RDesc:(ASCII, Ans) indicates that the response to the follow-up query is strongly influenced by the pre-viously generated output. Despite this notable improvement in consistency, answer accuracy in-creases only marginally. This limited gain may partly reflect to the suboptimal quality of the gener-ated ASCII representations, which correlates with lower answer accuracy (Table 2). Nevertheless, consistency remains a valuable in-dicator, particularly as it requires no ground-truth annotations for either layout or the answer. While prior work has applied Natural Language Inference (NLI) to assess reasoning via consistency (Wang et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023; Kumar and Taluk-dar, 2020), our approach benefits from the explicit verifiability of the generated artifact. Unlike un-structured text, ASCII representations enforce strict spatial constraints and encode the full spatial con-figuration, not just the queried pair, allowing us to objectively assess the alignment between the representation and the answer. We observe that when the model’s ASCII representation and its tex-tual answer agree, the answer is far more likely to be correct. This consistency check requires no ground-truth labels: the ASCII itself is structurally verifiable, so agreement between the two outputs serves as an unsupervised reliability signal. Our results show “ASCII as a Verifier” as a promising direction. The structural constraints im-posed by ASCII suggest that cross-representation consistency may provide a more reliable signal of reasoning validity than text-only self-consistency. The bottleneck was not the representation format but sketch quality : even af-ter fine-tuning, ground-truth ASCII ( RDesc+ASCII )still outperforms model-generated sketches, con-firming that further improvements in construction will yield additional reasoning gains. The persistent advantage of externally provided layouts, even after construction training, parallels human spatial cognition. In a preliminary human validation (Appendix I), participants achieve sub-stantially higher accuracy with access to ASCII layouts than with text descriptions alone. Mentally reconstructing spatial structure from sequential text is inherently more demanding than reading a pro-vided layout for both humans and models. The consistency analysis (Table 3) tells a com-plementary story. Construction training improves consistency between the model’s sketch and its an-swer far more than comprehension training does. The mechanism is intuitive: when the sketch is ac-curate, it reinforces the textual answer; when it re-mains flawed, better comprehension only makes the model more aware of the conflict between its noisy sketch and the description. Construction training thus both improves consistency and ensures that consistent instances are more likely to be correct. The confusion matrices (Appendix, Figure 4) cor-roborate this: construction training reduces confu-sions between spatially adjacent directions, sharp-ening directional boundaries. Table 4 tests whether these gains extend be-yond T EXT 2S PACE by evaluating on three external benchmarks under zero-shot prompting, without ASCII at inference time. Both configurations that include construction training (FT D→A and FT D↔A)improve on all three benchmarks, while comprehen-sion training alone does not, confirming construc-tion as the necessary ingredient for transfer. Bidi-rectional training further improves over construc-tion alone on two of three benchmarks, with 3.6 per-centage points on BABI task 19 and 5.7 percentage points on S PART QA, showing that comprehension further strengthens the foundations of spatial cog-nition that construction provides. Comprehension training (FT D←A) on its own shows an inconsistent pattern: it degrades S TEP GAME , which tests pre-cise directional navigation, yet yields large gains on S PART QA, which emphasizes parsing complex spatial language. This aligns with each task’s de-mands and shows that comprehension alone cannot substitute for construction. 

6 Conclusion 

We investigate whether training language models to construct explicit spatial layouts can instill gen-uine spatial understanding. Using ASCII grids as a diagnostic instrument that is text-native, human-readable, and structurally verifiable, we introduce TEXT 2S PACE , a benchmark to separate construc-tion failures from reasoning failures. Our results reveal a clear read-write asymme-try. Models can interpret spatial layouts effectively but struggle to construct them from text, and these construction errors propagate to downstream rea-soning. Consistency analysis further shows that when a model’s ASCII representation agrees with its textual answer, answer accuracy is substantially higher. This agreement serves as an unsupervised reliability signal. Training models to construct layouts addresses this bottleneck directly: spatial reasoning from text improves even without active sketching at inference time, and the benefit trans-fers to three external benchmarks (S TEP GAME ,

> B

ABI, S PART QA) that require spatial understand-ing. Only configurations that include construction training generalize across all three benchmarks, with the combination of construction and compre-hension yielding the strongest gains. These re-sults indicate that the spatial understanding gained through layout construction is not format-specific but foundational, enabling models to reason more effectively across diverse task formulations. 8Limitations 

ASCII grids are one instantiation of our approach, chosen for diagnostic precision and structural ver-ifiability. This scope entails several boundaries: we do not explore alternative formats such as coor-dinate lists or scene graphs, nor spatial problems involving continuous geometry, pixel-level percep-tion, or 3D environments. As a discrete abstraction, ASCII does not capture the richness of continuous spatial perception, and our synthetic benchmark may not fully represent real-world noise and am-biguity. Future work could examine whether con-struction training transfers to other model families and whether richer formats scale to more complex spatial environments. Our evaluation relies on answer accuracy and consistency between generated diagrams and pre-dictions. These metrics provide observable, ver-ifiable signals of reasoning behavior but do not directly reveal the model’s internal representations. While ASCII’s structure offers a foundation for it-erative verification and self-correction, this work does not explore such mechanisms.

23: else if Type is "Horizontal" then 

24: if x1 < x 2 then Rel inf ← "left" 25: else if x1 > x 2 then Rel inf ← "right" 26: else Rel inf ← "same column" 27: end if 

28: else ▷ Full Spatial Query 29: Compute vector ⃗v = ( x 1 − x2, y 1 − y2)

30: Rel inf ← Map ⃗v to {upper-left, below, right, ...} 31: end if 4. Compare with Reference Label 

32: M atch ← (LowerCase (Rel inf ) == LowerCase (L)) 

33: R ← { Pass : M atch, Exp : L, Act : Rel inf }
